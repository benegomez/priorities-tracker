CRS Product Strategy

Executive Summary

The Commitment Reliability Score (CRS) is the cornerstone of Priorities Tracker and the primary differentiator that defines the product’s market position.

Most productivity and project management platforms focus on tracking work, activities, tasks, or project progress. While valuable, these tools rarely answer a more important managerial question:

How reliable is a person when it comes to delivering on commitments?

CRS was created to answer that question.

The score provides a measurable and objective view of execution reliability by analyzing how consistently an individual fulfills the commitments they make over time.

CRS transforms Priorities Tracker from a simple planning tool into a Commitment Reliability Management platform.

⸻

The Problem CRS Solves

Managers are typically exposed to large amounts of activity data:

* Tasks completed
* Tickets closed
* Hours worked
* Meetings attended
* Messages exchanged

However, activity does not necessarily indicate reliability.

A highly active individual may consistently fail to complete their commitments, while another person may deliver predictably with fewer visible activities.

Organizations often lack a standardized way to measure:

* Commitment fulfillment
* Consistency of execution
* Reliability over time
* Early indicators of execution risk

As a result:

* Performance conversations become subjective.
* Coaching opportunities are harder to identify.
* Risk detection occurs too late.
* Execution predictability suffers.

CRS addresses this gap.

⸻

Strategic Positioning

Most tools answer:

What is someone working on?

Some tools answer:

How much work has been completed?

CRS answers:

How reliably does someone fulfill the commitments they make?

This distinction creates a fundamentally different value proposition.

Priorities Tracker does not compete primarily on project management features.

It competes on accountability visibility and execution reliability.

⸻

Product Philosophy

The CRS is based on several core beliefs.

Commitments Matter More Than Activity

People should be evaluated based on the commitments they make and their ability to fulfill them.

Activity alone is insufficient.

⸻

Consistency Matters More Than Individual Successes

A person who consistently delivers 85% of commitments may be more reliable than someone who alternates between 100% and 40%.

Predictability is valuable.

⸻

Reliability Can Be Measured

Execution reliability is not purely subjective.

Patterns of commitment and completion can be quantified.

⸻

Reliability Improves Through Visibility

When people can see their historical performance and managers can identify trends early, coaching becomes more effective.

⸻

CRS Inputs

The score is calculated using information generated throughout the weekly planning cycle.

Priorities Committed

Number of priorities included during Check-In.

⸻

Priorities Completed

Number of priorities completed during Check-Out.

⸻

Tasks Committed

Number of tasks associated with committed priorities.

⸻

Tasks Completed

Number of completed tasks.

⸻

Carried-Over Priorities

Number of priorities moved to subsequent weeks.

Repeated carry-over may indicate planning or execution issues.

⸻

Historical Consistency

Long-term behavior across multiple planning cycles.

This prevents short-term fluctuations from disproportionately impacting the score.

⸻

Interpretation Model

The CRS should be easy to understand.

Score Range	Interpretation
90-100	Excellent Reliability
80-89	Strong Reliability
70-79	Preventive Attention Required
Below 70	Active Follow-Up Recommended

Risk Considerations

Risk 1: Perceived Surveillance

Mitigation

Position CRS as a coaching and alignment tool.

Not a monitoring tool.

⸻

Risk 2: Gaming the System

Mitigation

Use multiple variables rather than a single completion percentage.

⸻

Risk 3: Overemphasis on Score

Mitigation

CRS should complement managerial judgment rather than replace it.
